This multicenter study examined whether standardized cognitive, motor, and sensory assessments from the NIH Toolbox could predict brain amyloid status in older adults. Using machine learning (random forest and LASSO logistic regression) applied to NIHTB scores and demographic data from 199 participants (mean age 76.3 years; 51.3% female; 33.2% amyloid positive), the random forest model achieved superior performance with an AUROC of 0.74 (95% CI: 0.73–0.76, sensitivity 0.50, specificity 0.88). Top predictive features included picture sequence memory, overall cognition composite score, fluid cognition measures, hearing assessments, processing speed, odor identification, and motor endurance. These findings support the utilization of the NIH Toolbox as an efficient and standardizable Alzheimer's disease biomarker measurement tool and may inform clinical eligibility determination for anti-amyloid therapies.
