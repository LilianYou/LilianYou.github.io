Research Vision

I develop computational and multi-modal neuroimaging approaches to understand the mechanisms underlying brain disorders and aging. My long-term goal is to build a research program that bridges mechanistic neuroscience and clinical translation — using machine learning and data fusion to move from understanding what changes in the brain to understanding why, and ultimately to inform early detection and intervention.

My research draws on training in cognitive neuroscience, computational modeling, and clinical neuroimaging. While Alzheimer's disease and related dementias (ADRD) are my primary focus, the methods and frameworks I develop are designed to generalize across psychiatric conditions, neurological disorders, and normative aging.

Multi-modal Neuroimaging & Data Fusion

A central challenge in clinical neuroscience is that no single data modality captures the full complexity of brain disorders. My research develops computational frameworks for integrating multiple data streams — structural and functional MRI, fluid biomarkers, electronic health records, and cognitive assessments — to build richer, mechanistically grounded models of disease.

I am particularly interested in understanding why certain multi-modal patterns predict clinical outcomes, not just that they do. This mechanistic focus distinguishes my approach from purely predictive machine learning, and opens the door to identifying intervention targets.

Computational Approaches to Neurodegeneration

Alzheimer's disease and related dementias unfold over decades, with heterogeneous trajectories across individuals. My work uses machine learning and neuroimaging to characterize these trajectories — identifying subtypes, predicting progression, and understanding the biological factors that drive cognitive decline.

At MGH/MADRC, I contributed to developing robust tools for large-scale neuroimaging analysis (including SynthSeg+, published in PNAS) and applied NLP methods to clinical text data. My current work at McLean/HMS focuses on neuropsychiatric symptoms in ADRD, normative modeling, and multi-modal data fusion approaches to understanding heterogeneity in neurodegeneration.

Spatial Navigation & Cognitive Neuroscience

My doctoral research established that travel direction is a fundamental and independent component of human navigation — distinct from the well-studied head direction signal. Using fMRI and novel psychophysical paradigms, I showed that the brain maintains multiple, dynamic spatial representations that shift over the course of learning, challenging the dominant framework of primarily allocentric coding.

This foundational work on how healthy brains compute and navigate space is now informing new directions in clinical translation. Spatial navigation is among the earliest cognitive domains affected in Alzheimer's disease, and the paradigms I developed for studying healthy navigation can be repurposed as sensitive cognitive assessments and potentially as targets for cognitive training and intervention.

Individual Differences Across Populations

A thread running through all my research is attention to individual differences — across sex, culture, age, and clinical status. During my doctoral work, I examined how handedness and biological sex influence spatial cognition using both human participants and neurorobotic models, and conducted large-scale analyses of navigation behavior across countries using data from nearly 770,000 participants.

This population-level perspective now informs my clinical work: understanding who is most affected by neurodegeneration, and why, is essential for developing equitable and effective interventions.
